# Product

## Register

product

## Users

All active firefighters and officers in a volunteer fire department — ranging from tech-comfortable younger members to 50+ year-old rural crew members who may rarely use software beyond a phone. The fire chief or department administrator is the buyer; the entire crew is the daily user base.

Users open this app in high-stress, time-pressured moments: mid-shift, post-call at 2am, in a apparatus bay with gloved hands. They are not sitting at a desk. They want to complete their task and get out.

## Product Purpose

A mission-critical operations platform for volunteer fire departments. Covers incident logging (NERIS 2026 compliance), apparatus status tracking, asset management (PPE, SCBA), offline-first field use, and regulatory compliance.

Success looks like: a firefighter logs a complete incident report in under five minutes with no training required. An ISO auditor finds every required record present and properly formatted. A chief runs the department from a phone.

## Brand Personality

Dependable. Precise. Unobtrusive.

The product earns trust by never surprising the user. It does not celebrate itself. It gets out of the way.

## Anti-references

- No SaaS-bold aesthetics: oversized hero metrics, gradient accents, "delight-first" motion.
- No consumer app patterns: bottom nav tabs, card carousels, onboarding mascots.
- No startup minimalism that mistakes sparse for serious.
- Paladin Fire and similar legacy fire software: dated, cluttered, visually inconsistent.
- Generic admin dashboard templates: navy sidebars, identical card grids, hero-metric blocks.

The reference aesthetic is closer to government digital services (GOV.UK, USDS) and professional field tools (Procore, ServiceMax) — polished, institutional, nothing wasted.

## Design Principles

1. **Zero training required.** Every label, action, and state must be immediately legible to someone who has never seen the screen before. Plain English always; no jargon, no acronyms without expansion.
2. **Reliability is the visual language.** Consistency, predictability, and clear feedback over novelty. If an interaction surprises the user, it is a bug.
3. **Designed for stress.** Assume the user is tired, cold, or in a hurry. Favor large targets, high contrast, and single-step actions. Never make the user read to act.
4. **Information hierarchy over decoration.** Every visual element must serve a task. Ornament that does not communicate is removed.
5. **Institutional gravity.** Polished but not slick. The product should feel like it belongs in a government operations center, not a startup demo.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum across all surfaces. Strong contrast ratios preferred (target 4.5:1 body text, 3:1 large text). 44px minimum touch targets everywhere. Reduced motion support via `prefers-reduced-motion`. No color-only status communication — always pair color with label or icon. Font sizes legible without zoom on a standard phone.
